Few-Shot Fine-Grained Radiolarian Fossil Identification Using DINOv3: Insights into Permian-Triassic Mass Extinction Dynamics
1.Introduction
1.1 Radiolarians and their significance in the Permian–Triassic mass extinction
Radiolarians are unicellular marine protists that are widely distributed throughout the modern ocean. They are characterized by intricate siliceous skeletons that contribute to buoyancy and provide protection in the planktonic realm (De Wever et al. 2002, Anderson 2012). As members of the supergroup Rhizaria, radiolarians display remarkable morphological diversity—including spherical, conical, and multisegmented forms—and their opaline silica frameworks are readily preserved as microfossils in deep-sea sediments (Anderson 2012). The radiolarian fossil record extends from the Cambrian to the present, offering critical insights into paleoceanographic conditions and evolutionary patterns across geological timescales (De Wever et al. 2002).
During the Permian–Triassic (P–T) mass extinction at approximately 252 Ma, radiolarians experienced catastrophic losses in diversity, with more than 90% of species disappearing, mirroring profound oceanic anoxia and widespread environmental disruption (Isozaki et al. 2007). Radiolarian assemblages across the P–T boundary capture a pronounced faunal turnover: Albaillellaria dominate in the Late Permian, followed by recovery communities in the Early Triassic in which Entactinaria and Nassellaria become prevalent, underscoring adaptive responses to a global crisis (Feng et al. 2007). These microfossil records serve as proxies for reconstructing paleoenvironmental change—including ocean acidification and a collapse in primary productivity—processes implicated in the most severe biotic crisis in Earth history (Payne et al. 2004). In particular, radiolarian cherts from deep-water successions in regions such as South China and Japan preserve continuous P–T boundary archives, enabling high-resolution biostratigraphic correlation and yielding insights into extinction selectivity and post-extinction recovery dynamics (Isozaki et al. 2007).

During the Permian–Triassic (P–T) mass extinction (~252 Ma), radiolarians underwent catastrophic biodiversity loss exceeding 90%, synchronous with global oceanic anoxia and rapid perturbations in biogeochemical cycling(Isozaki et al. 2007, Shen et al. 2019). Recent geochemical and sedimentological studies reveal that extensive volcanism and volcanic ash deposition intensified redox instability, sustaining prolonged anoxia and delaying marine recovery (Zhang et al. 2025). Across the P–T boundary, radiolarian assemblages record a pronounced faunal turnover: Albaillellaria-dominated Late Permian communities gave way to Early Triassic Entactinaria–Nassellaria associations, reflecting ecological adaptation to nutrient limitation and episodic oxygenation events(Feng and Algeo 2014, Al Aswad et al. 2025).
Radiolarian cherts from continuous deep-water successions in South China and the Panthalassan/Tethyan realm provide high-resolution archives of extinction selectivity and recovery dynamics, revealing close coupling between microfossil turnover, redox oscillations, and the collapse of primary productivity during the P–T crisis (Isozaki et al. 2007, Feng and Algeo 2014, Shen et al. 2019).


Recent studies further suggest that, across the P–T transition, radiolarians did not exhibit a uniform size response to climatic change and extinction, highlighting climatic cooling as a key control (Xiao et al. 2025). Radiolarian evolutionary trajectories also indicate a staged collapse through the extinction interval, with differentiated ecosystem responses across paleogeographic settings (Huang et al. 2023). In the Late Permian, radiolarians underwent severe crises at both species and genus levels, emphasizing the dynamic interplay between survival and recovery phases (Feng et al. 2007). Work in Paleozoic radiolarian biostratigraphy has additionally clarified the marked post-event increase in biodiversity, shedding light on mechanisms of recovery after the P–T catastrophe (Xiao et al. 2021). A large database analysis spanning the Middle Permian to Middle Triassic likewise reports no consistent coupling between radiolarian body size and climate change across the Paleozoic–Mesozoic transition, challenging prevailing assumptions that size shifts track environmental stress in a predictable manner (Xiao et al. 2025). The deterioration and collapse of Late Permian marine ecosystems appear to reflect complex, multi-causal processes that may include still underexplored drivers such as volcanism and fluctuations in redox conditions (He et al. 2025). High-resolution radiolarian records through the P–T interval thus provide a powerful basis for evaluating planktonic community resilience, particularly within standardized low-latitude biostratigraphic frameworks (Xiao et al. 2018). Collectively, these findings underscore the value of radiolarians for revealing selective extinction patterns and survivor biases during global biotic crises (Foster et al. 2022), and for elucidating facets of ecosystem resilience—for example, the role of opportunistic taxa in repopulating depauperate oceans following extinction (Feng and Algeo 2014).

1.2 Applications of artificial intelligence in palaeontology
Here we provide a focused overview of AI applications in palaeontology, emphasizing image-based microfossil identification and the data-regime shift from supervised learning to self-/semi-supervised representation learning, to motivate our few-shot radiolarian case study.
Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly reshaping data-driven workflows in palaeontology, automating tasks such as fossil classification, segmentation, and predictive modelling since the 1980s (Liu and Song 2020, Marques et al. 2025). Early efforts in the field relied on knowledge-based systems and neural networks for microfossil recognition; however, persistent obstacles in data compilation and model deployment meant that palaeontological applications lagged behind mainstream AI development by roughly one to two decades (Yu et al. 2024). In micropalaeontology, AI approaches—most notably convolutional neural networks (CNNs)—have been used to classify foraminifera and ostracods, achieving high accuracy at the genus level, albeit typically contingent on access to large, expertly annotated datasets (Hsiang et al. 2019).
For radiolarians, recent studies have adopted transfer learning with architectures such as ResNet and vision transformers (ViTs) for image-based classification, demonstrating the feasibility of automated taxonomic workflows in assemblages from intervals such as the Middle Eocene and the Cretaceous (Carlsson et al. 2023, Mimura et al. 2025). Nevertheless, much of this progress has been concentrated at relatively coarse taxonomic ranks (e.g., family or genus), and success remains limited for fine-grained species-level discrimination because diagnostic differences are often subtle and training data are scarce (Ranaweera et al. 2009, Wang et al. 2022). For example, microscopy-based pipelines have been developed that integrate image acquisition, focus stacking, preprocessing, automated segmentation, and subsequent CNN-based recognition, substantially reducing the labour and time required for manual slide screening; yet inter-operator variability in identification and labelling can still introduce inconsistencies that compromise reproducibility (Tetard et al. 2020, Martinsen et al. 2024).
Across palaeontology more broadly, AI has advanced applications ranging from macrofossil prediction to phylogenetic reconstruction, whereas micropalaeontological use remains comparatively underrepresented; in a recent survey, fewer than 20% of reviewed studies addressed protistan groups such as radiolarians (Yu et al. 2024). Despite notable gains, much of the current literature continues to rely predominantly on supervised learning paradigms, with limited integration of self-supervised representation learning, thereby constraining robustness to fossil heterogeneity and preservation-related variability (Marchant et al. 2020, Rodgers 2025). This methodological gap is especially consequential in the data-limited settings that typify ancient microfossil research, and it underscores the need for approaches capable of improving fine-grained recognition under severe annotation constraints (Mitra et al. 2019). More recently, AI-based radiolarian studies have also begun to extend beyond identification toward evolutionary interpretation—for instance, using machine-learning models to quantify morphological change in Mesozoic radiolarians (Carlsson et al. 2023). Collectively, these developments indicate that AI can not only accelerate data processing, but also reveal patterns that may be difficult to capture using conventional workflows, including diversity dynamics across major transitions such as the P–T boundary interval (Yu et al. 2024). However, reliable species-level identification of radiolarians remains difficult in few-shot fossil datasets, where diagnostically relevant differences are subtle, preservation is variable, and image distributions differ markedly across published sources—conditions that collectively limit the transferability of standard supervised pipelines. These constraints motivate representation-learning approaches that can exploit unlabeled target-domain images to build morphology-sensitive features before scarce labels are introduced.
This study explores the feasibility of few-shot, fine-grained radiolarian fossil identification using self-supervised vision transformers, with a focus on the Permian–Triassic boundary interval. We compile a literature-derived image dataset spanning 281 taxa compiled from published plates across multiple sources and propose a cascaded transfer-learning workflow that integrates baseline evaluation, task-adaptive pre-training (TAPT) for domain alignment, and progressive supervised fine-tuning. We assess performance under extreme per-class data limitations and discuss how scalable identification may support quantitative analyses of radiolarian turnover and recovery dynamics across the P–T crisis.

2. Methods
2.1 Data collection
Radiolarian fossil images were compiled by extracting specimens from published literature. Focusing on the Permian–Triassic mass extinction as the primary temporal framework, we assembled an image dataset comprising 281 radiolarian taxa, dominated by Albaillellaria. For each taxon, 15 images were collected, yielding a total of >4,000 images.
2.2 DINOv3
In this study, we adopt DINOv3 (Distillation with No Labels v3), recently introduced by Meta AI, as the backbone visual encoder for feature extraction(Siméoni et al. 2025). DINOv3 reflects a broader shift in computer vision from purely label-driven semantic learning toward structured self-supervised representation learning(Caron et al. 2021). In the context of palaeontological classification, DINOv3 is used not merely as a generic feature extractor, but as a framework for learning morphology-sensitive representations that may capture taxonomically informative invariances in microfossil imagery(Rodgers 2025, Yaqoob et al. 2025). Its principal strength lies in leveraging large-scale unlabeled data to learn robust visual features without relying on extensive human annotation or strong prior assumptions. Concretely, through a combination of discriminative self-distillation and generative masked-image modeling, DINOv3 can induce representations that encode texture cues, edge topology, and higher-order geometric structure that are prevalent in radiolarian microfossil images.

2.2.1 Discriminative Self-Distillation Architecture
The core architecture of DINOv3 is predicated on an asymmetric Knowledge Distillation mechanism, comprising two neural networks—a Student network (g_{\theta_s}) and a Teacher network (g_{\theta_t})—that share an identical topological structure (Vision Transformer) yet employ distinct parameter update strategies.
To emulate the cognitive process of taxonomists who infer global morphology from local details, DINOv3 employs a Multi-view Consistency training strategy. For any given radiolarian image x, the algorithm constructs two distinct sets of views: Global Views (x_g), which encompass the overall silhouette, and Local Views (x_l), which contain only partial crops. The mathematical objective compels the Student network to infer feature distributions consistent with the Teacher network’s observation of the global view (e.g., the complete radial skeleton) relying solely on local views (e.g., local mesh pore structures). This mechanism forces the model to capture the intrinsic high-dimensional correlations between fine-grained local textures and global morphology.
Formally, the network outputs are mapped to probability distributions P_s and P_t via a Softmax function with a temperature parameter:
P_s\left(x\right)=\mathrm{Softmax}\left(\frac{g_{\theta_s}\left(x\right)}{\tau_s}\right),\emsp P_t\left(x\right)=\mathrm{Softmax}\left(\frac{g_{\theta_t}\left(x\right)-c}{\tau_t}\right)
where \tau_s and \tau_t serve as temperature coefficients regulating the sharpness of the distributions, and c denotes the Sinkhorn-Knopp centering parameter designed to prevent representation collapse. The optimization objective is to minimize the cross-entropy loss between the Student and Teacher distributions:
\operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{DINO}}=\min\below{\theta_s}{\sum_{x\in\{x_g\}}{\sum_{x^\prime\in V_{local}}{-P_t\left(x\right)}\log{P_s}\left(x^\prime\right)}}
To ensure the stability of feature acquisition, the parameters of the Teacher network, \theta_t, are not updated via gradient backpropagation. Instead, they evolve temporally through an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of the Student parameters \theta_s:
\theta_t\gets\lambda\theta_t+\left(1-\lambda\right)\theta_s
This momentum-based update mechanism functions as Temporal Ensembling, providing the model with a more robust guidance signal for feature alignment.
2.2.2 Masked Modeling and Structural Reconstruction
Addressing common geological noise interferences in microfossils (e.g., fragmentation and sedimentary occlusion), DINOv3 innovatively integrates the Masked Image Modeling (MIM) mechanism derived from the iBOT framework. This mechanism introduces an auxiliary generative task: randomly masking image patches and requiring the model to reconstruct the latent features of the occluded regions based on the remaining contextual information.
Mathematically, for an input image tokenized into a sequence of patches, a random binary mask M is applied. The objective is to minimize the cross-entropy between the Student network's prediction on the masked view \hat{x} and the Teacher network's projection of the original unmasked image x, specifically over the masked indices i\ \in M:
\operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{MIM}}=-\sum_{i\in M}{P_t\left(x\right)_i}\cdot\log{P_s}\left(\hat{x}\right)_i
This enhancement confers a dual advantage upon the model:
Enhanced Robustness: The model is compelled to internalize priors regarding the continuity and symmetry of radiolarian skeletons, thereby acquiring the capability to infer complete morphologies from incomplete specimens.
Fine-Grained Perception: To accurately reconstruct occluded high-frequency details, the model must focus its attention on the most discriminative micro-structures (e.g., shell wall layering and spine base morphology).
Furthermore, to maximize the uniformity of the feature space and prevent cluster collapse, DINOv3 incorporates the KoLeo regularization term. Based on the Kozachenko-Leonenko differential entropy estimator, this term encourages the feature vectors $z$ within a batch of size $N$ to be equidistant, defined as:
\operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{KoLeo}}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\ln{\left(\min\below{j\neq i}{|}z_i-z_j|\right)}
Crucially, to resolve the degradation of high-resolution feature maps in deep network layers, the architecture employs a Gram Anchoring technique. This imposes a constraint on the global correlation structure of the patch tokens. By computing the Gram matrix G, where G_{ij} represents the uncentered covariance between feature dimensions, the model enforces consistency between the Student and Teacher representations at a structural level:
\operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{Gram}}=\frac{1}{D^2}|G\left(z_s\right)-G\left(z_t\right)|_F^2
where |\cdot|_F denotes the Frobenius norm. This effectively ensures the preservation of high-frequency information essential for microscopic image analysis.

2.3 Technical workflow
Given the characteristics of our radiolarian fossil image dataset—a strictly balanced class composition (15 images per class) yet an extreme few-shot regime per taxon, coupled with fine-grained inter-class morphological differences and substantial domain shift from natural images—we do not rely on a naive “direct fine-tuning” strategy. Instead, we propose a cascaded transfer-learning framework comprising three stages: baseline evaluation, task-adaptive pre-training (TAPT), and progressive supervised fine-tuning. The core idea is to pre-align the representation manifold using self-supervised signals from the target domain, thereby maximizing feature task-relevance before introducing scarce human annotations.
2.3.1 Baseline construction and evaluation
Before adaptation, we establish a baseline using DINOv3 weights pretrained on ImageNet-22k. Because microscopy-based palaeontological imagery differs markedly from natural-image source domains, direct transfer may yield suboptimal representations. We therefore evaluate the pretrained feature space using lightweight, non-parametric classifiers—specifically k-nearest neighbours (k-NN) and a prototype-based classifier—under zero-shot and few-shot settings. This step quantifies the lower-bound performance attainable with generic representations and provides a reference point for assessing the gains attributable to domain adaptation.
2.3.2 Task-adaptive pre-training (TAPT) for domain alignment
To mitigate domain shift at the representation level, we employ task-adaptive pre-training (TAPT). Rather than introducing additional external corpora of uncertain relevance, we perform continued self-supervised learning directly on the target radiolarian dataset with labels removed, ensuring that adaptation is driven by the exact distribution encountered in the downstream task.
Concretely, the model is initialized from generic pretrained weights and then trained further on the target domain using the asymmetric self-distillation objective and the iBOT masked reconstruction mechanism described in Section 2.2. This process can be interpreted as unsupervised manifold alignment, encouraging the encoder to shift attention away from natural-image regularities (e.g., colour statistics and macroscopic object cues) and toward radiolarian-specific morphological signals (e.g., spine architecture, pore-lattice organization, and segmentation patterns). Through TAPT, the representation is reshaped to better reflect the morphological structure of radiolarians prior to any exposure to labels, thereby providing a stronger substrate for subsequent supervised learning in a per-class few-shot setting.
2.3.3 Progressive Supervised Fine-Tuning
Leveraging the domain-adapted weights, a progressive freeze-unfreeze protocol is implemented to balance rapid adaptation with stability, thereby mitigating the risk of catastrophic forgetting.
Linear Probing. Initially, the Vision Transformer backbone f_\theta is frozen, and only a linear classification head h_\phi is trained. This phase isolates the quality of the learned representations and allows for the rapid calibration of decision boundaries without perturbing the underlying feature extractor. The optimization objective is defined as:
\min\below\phi{E_{\left(x,y\right)\sim\mathbb{D}}}\left[\operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{CE}}\left(h_\phi\left(f_\theta\left(x\right)\right),y\right)\right]
where \theta remains fixed, and \operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{CE}} denotes the Cross-Entropy loss.
Full Fine-Tuning with Layer-wise Learning-Rate Decay (LLRD). Subsequently, the entire network is unfrozen and fine-tuned end-to-end using Layer-wise Learning-Rate Decay (LLRD). Acknowledging that shallower ViT layers encode general, low-level texture primitives while deeper layers capture high-level, task-specific abstractions, distinct learning rates are assigned to each layer. Specifically, the learning rate \eta_l for the l-th layer is computed as:
\eta_l=\eta_{base}\cdot\gamma^{L-l}
where \eta_{base} is the learning rate of the final layer, L is the total number of layers, and \gamma is the decay factor (set to 0.75). This strategy preserves the stability of early-layer representations while facilitating plasticity in higher layers for task adaptation.
Regularization and Augmentation. To enhance generalization under conditions of severe per-class data scarcity, Mixup and CutMix augmentations are integrated with a Soft Target Cross-Entropy objective. For a generated virtual sample \widetilde{x} constructed from inputs x_i,x_j with a mixing coefficient \lambda, the smoothed target label is defined as \widetilde{y}=\lambda y_i+\left(1-\lambda\right)y_j. The loss function minimizes the divergence between the predicted probability distribution P\left(\widetilde{x}\right) and this soft target:
\operatorname{L}_{\mathcal{Soft}}=-\sum_{k}\widetilde{y_k}\log{P}\left(\widetilde{x}\right)_k
This approach smoothes the decision manifold and reduces model overconfidence, serving as a critical safeguard against overfitting in fine-grained, few-shot recognition scenarios.

3. Results
3.1 Self-supervised pre-training
To systematically examine how the amount of target-domain data affects representation quality during task-adaptive pre-training (Task-Adaptive Pre-training, TAPT), we conducted a controlled set of experiments with varying data proportions. Specifically, from the full dataset of radiolarian fossil images (>4,000 images), we randomly subsampled five training subsets corresponding to 0% (i.e., directly using ImageNet-22k pretrained weights with no target-domain self-supervised adaptation), 20%, 50%, 80%, and 100%, and performed self-supervised pre-training on each subset.
All runs followed an identical DINOv3 training recipe—including discriminative self-distillation with multi-view consistency, iBOT-style masked image modeling, KoLeo regularization, and Gram-matrix anchoring—and we kept the key hyperparameters fixed across conditions.
